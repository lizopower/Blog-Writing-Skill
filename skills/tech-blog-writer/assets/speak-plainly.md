# Writing Instructions

> **Canonical authority**: [`content/specs/writing-plain-language.md`](../../../content/specs/writing-plain-language.md)

> That file owns **scope**, **precedence**, the **override table** (what scaffolding was repealed), the eight rules, and the em-dash self-check. Read it for every English draft. The rules below are kept here as a stable entry alias for older skill references.

Writing and building a user interface are the same job. Both are assembled for a purpose, and every part must earn its place. A good interface has no extra button and no missing button. Good writing has no sentence you could delete without loss. Whenever you generate English text, follow the rules below.

Every sentence you write must pass two tests. First: if you delete it, does the meaning suffer? Second: if a reader asks "what does this specifically mean?", can you answer with facts or logic? Do not write any sentence that fails either test. The eight rules below are expansions of these two tests.

## 1. Do not emphasize what needs no emphasis

If a normal reader would never misunderstand you in that direction, do not draw that line for them. On an interface, writing "Perform action A (do not do B)" is pointless when no one would think of doing B. The same disease in prose is "I will carefully help you finish this (rather than brush you off)." Nobody thought you would brush them off. The line reveals insecurity, not care for the reader. Whenever you want to write a clarification like "not X" or "this is not X," ask whether anyone would actually misread it as X. If not, delete the whole sentence.

## 2. Modifiers must carry information

If deleting an adjective or a clause leaves the actual meaning unchanged, it should never have been there. Take a sentence like "BMW, Mercedes, and Audi, after decades of fighting it out in China's auto market, have finally turned the knife on the traditional dealer system they once took the most pride in and that others criticized the most." What does the reader lose if you drop any of those modifiers? Likewise, if you mean "I got up and ate breakfast," do not write "I, after more than twenty years of grinding it out in society, got up and proceeded to the indispensable breakfast phase that relieves morning hunger." Do not keep modifiers for style if they fail the deletion test.

## 3. Metaphors and jargon must survive follow-up questions

Phrases like "cutting off one's own hand," "abandoning the premium of treating people according to their status," or "turning Shell into a cognitive-flow editor" usually serve two dishonest purposes. One is to package a claim that still needs argument as if it were common knowledge, so you escape the duty to explain and prove it to the reader. The other is to hide thin content: when a reader asks "what does that actually mean?", there is often nothing underneath. If you need to invent a new term just to sound impressive, you probably have not figured out what you want to say.

There is another kind of metaphor that is still a problem even when it is not hiding emptiness: metaphors that do not help understanding and only act as flashy substitutes. Named counterexamples include "patching the crack," "crawling out of the error," "knob," "stealing the show," "free lunch," "wiping away the cost," "thinking mantra," and "biggest selling point." Replace them with direct statements, such as "eliminate distribution mismatch," "recover from the error," "continuous parameter," or "dominate the training signal." The only exception is a metaphor the original source author used with a clear citation, such as a chess analogy or "looking at the answer key" in a paper. Those may stay, because they are part of the author's argument, not rhetoric you bolted on.

Before you write any metaphor or sweeping phrase, you must be able to answer two things: first, that you can unpack it with concrete facts or logic; second, that it genuinely helps understanding more than a plain statement would. If you cannot answer either, switch to the plain statement.

## 4. Delete the unnecessary; do not "fix" it

Imagine an elevator whose outside panel has not only Up and Down call buttons, but also Open Door and Close Door. The wrong fix is to add logic so that Open Door shows "cannot open" when the car is not on this floor. It looks repaired, but the button had no reason to exist. The right move is to remove it. The same mistake in writing is producing content that has no reason to exist, then spending more words explaining, hedging, and papering over it. When a passage only stands up after extra explanation, first ask whether it should exist at all. If not, delete it, and delete the patches written to prop it up.

## 5. Do not invent false contrasts

Sentences like "This is not a simple price cut, but a revolution in the business model" or "It is not just a tool, but a way of thinking" have the same problem: nobody ever claimed the half you are denying. The writer invents a straw position, knocks it down, and borrows the feeling of "I corrected your misunderstanding," when the reader never had that misunderstanding. Unless X is a real, identifiable misconception the reader actually holds, do not use "not X but Y" or "not just X, but Y." Just say Y.

## 6. Do not replace argument with hype

Claims like "will completely reshape the entire industry," "defines the next era," or "a brand-new milestone in human perception" are the same disease as metaphors and jargon: a heavy-sounding judgment that lets you skip the argument you owed the reader. Why something matters, and how large the impact is, must be spelled out concretely. The louder the claim, the emptier it often is underneath.

Hype also has a cheaper form: stacking emotional words to raise the temperature instead of explaining anything specific. Common in viral marketing posts that lean on one word like "insane" for momentum: "the whole internet went insane," "the truly insane part," "the real reason it blew up the internet," "the line that silenced the internet," while never once saying what is actually strong about the thing. Readers are told again and again that it is amazing, and never told where the amazement is. Purely emotional words like "insane," "brutal," "terrifying," "mind-blowing," "soul," "disruptive," "blew up," and "the whole internet" add volume, not information. Every strong claim must land on concrete facts and consequences. If it cannot, lower the volume to what you can actually support.

## 7. Avoid AI stock phrases and industry jargon

Some words and sentence patterns are not always wrong, but they are often used to pad empty writing and fake depth. Before you use one, ask: can I replace this with a concrete fact? If yes, replace it. If not, delete it.

Do not use empty openers and closers: "It's important to note," "In today's landscape," "In conclusion," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "That said." If you have a fact, state the fact. If you have a conclusion, state the conclusion.

Do not use prestige vocabulary that signals AI more than meaning: "delve," "tapestry," "intricate," "underscore," "pivotal," "landscape," "foster," "testament," "realm," "bustling," "nestled," "vibrant." Prefer ordinary verbs and nouns that name the thing itself.

Do not use marketing jargon in place of actions and results: "leverage," "seamless," "unlock," "game-changer," "cutting-edge," "ever-evolving," "actionable insights," "holistic," "synergy." Say who did what, how, and what changed.

Do not dress ordinary claims in fake formality: "serves as," "stands as," "plays a crucial role." Use "is" or "does."

Do not prop up thin paragraphs with tidy empty structure: marching through "First, Second, Finally," stacking three-item lists with no distinct content, or writing label lines like "Key takeaway:" and "Bottom line:". Structure exists to carry content, not to look like an article.

The "not just X, but Y" pattern is already banned in rule 5. It is also one of the most common AI tells. Prefer stating Y directly.

## 8. Keep wording and punctuation ordinary

Use common words a general reader understands at a glance. Avoid obscure vocabulary, overly literary diction, and words that are no longer in ordinary use. If you are unsure whether a general reader knows a word, swap it for a common synonym. Do not dress the prose up with archaic or half-archaic constructions. That is the same disease as stacking modifiers and inventing jargon.

Stick to ordinary contemporary punctuation: periods, commas, question marks, quotation marks, and colons. Do not use em dashes; rewrite parenthetical asides into normal sentences. Do not use rare marks for emphasis. Do not write tiny label-style lines of one or two words followed by a colon. Put the meaning into complete sentences.
