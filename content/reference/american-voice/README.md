# American Voice — Speech, Oratory & Business-Prose Exemplars

Eight verified masterpieces of American English, chosen for **cadence, plainness,
and rhythm** — the qualities that make prose sound native. All sources fetched
and verified 2026-07-02.

**How these differ from `industrial-b2b/`**: these are voice-only references.
Oratory is NOT blog prose. Do **not** run `extract_genre_conventions.py` on this
folder, and do not copy oratorical devices wholesale into B2B drafts (see
"transfer rules" below).

## Transfer rules — what carries into B2B blogs, what doesn't

**Carries over (use freely):**
1. Plain Anglo-Saxon vocabulary. Jobs says "I returned Coke bottles for the
   five-cent deposits to buy food with" — not "I faced financial constraints."
   The concrete noun does the work (skill Rule 4, Rule 19).
2. Short declaratives at emotional/logical peaks. "That's it. No big deal.
   Just three stories." / "But it doesn't work. No airplanes land." (Rule 2).
3. Concrete detail over abstraction. Feynman's bamboo antennas and wooden
   headphones ARE the argument. Numbers and objects, not adjectives.
4. Direct address without hedging. "You've got to find what you love." No
   "one might consider finding".
5. Trust the reader: no signposting, no "let me explain why this matters."
   Jobs never says "the moral of this story is…" until the story has earned it.

**Does NOT carry over (oratory-only devices):**
1. Heavy parallelism/anaphora (JFK's "We choose to go to the Moon… not because
   they are easy, but because they are hard"). In a blog this reads as the
   AI-typical antithesis pattern your own lint flags (`AI-pattern`). Oratory
   earns repetition through a live audience; prose doesn't.
2. Grand cosmic framing ("in the face of death…"). B2B readers want the spec.
3. Emotional crescendo endings. Blog sections end by advancing (Rule 8).
