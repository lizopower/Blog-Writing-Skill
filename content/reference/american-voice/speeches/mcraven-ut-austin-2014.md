# "Make Your Bed" — Adm. McRaven, UT Austin, 2014

> **Why this maps directly onto the how-to genre**
> - Structure IS a how-to article: 10 numbered lessons, each a fixed unit of
>   [concrete SEAL-training anecdote] → [principle in one sentence] → [refrain].
>   That anecdote→rule unit is this skill's middle-paragraph pattern, verbatim.
> - Military plainness: imperative mood, no hedging, numbers everywhere
>   (150 men down to 35; six months; 8-to-10-foot surf; 25 obstacles).
> - Deadpan humor by precision: "That great paragon of analytical rigor,
>   Ask.Com" — the joke is IN the specificity.

## Structure skeleton

Opening (self-deprecating: can't remember his own commencement speaker) →
the 10×-compounding math (10 people × 5 generations = 800 million) →
ten lessons, each anecdote→principle→"If you want to change the world…" →
close (recap in plain imperatives, callback to the bell).

## Excerpts and what to steal

The mechanism of small wins, in chain logic:

> If you make your bed every morning you will have accomplished the first task
> of the day. It will give you a small sense of pride, and it will encourage
> you to do another task and another and another. […] If you can't do the
> little things right, you will never do the big things right.

Concrete numbers carry the drama:

> Over a few weeks of difficult training my SEAL class, which started with 150
> men, was down to just 35.

Failure normalized in four short sentences:

> Life is filled with circuses. You will fail. You will likely fail often. It
> will be painful.

Three-word paragraph as emphasis (used once):

> No one wanted a circus.

**Transfer notes**:
1. The anecdote→principle unit transfers to B2B how-to writing intact:
   [field failure story] → [design rule] is the same move.
2. The refrain ("If you want to change the world…" ×10) does NOT transfer —
   in prose it's the repetitive-template AI tell. One callback max.
