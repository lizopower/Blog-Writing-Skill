# "We choose to go to the Moon" — JFK, Rice University, 1962

> **Why it's here — and why it carries a warning label**
> - The most famous rhythm in American rhetoric. Read it aloud to calibrate
>   your ear for stress and cadence.
> - BUT: its signature device — balanced antithesis — is precisely the pattern
>   that reads as AI-generated in blog prose. Your own lint (`AI-pattern`,
>   `Not only X but also Y`) flags it. Learn the cadence; do not copy the device.

## Excerpts

The sentence everyone knows — antithesis earned by a stadium of 35,000:

> We choose to go to the Moon in this decade and do the other things, not
> because they are easy, but because they are hard.

What's less quoted — JFK grounds the grandeur in concrete engineering detail,
which IS transferable:

> …a giant rocket more than 300 feet tall, the length of this football field,
> made of new metal alloys, some of which have not yet been invented, capable
> of standing heat and stresses several times more than have ever been
> experienced, fitted together with a precision better than the finest watch…

**Transfer notes**:
1. The football-field comparison is the master move for B2B writing: convert
   an abstract spec (300 feet) into the reader's physical world. "The length
   of this football field" is Rule 4 (concrete nouns) at presidential scale.
2. "Some of which have not yet been invented" — honest uncertainty stated
   plainly, inside a confident sentence. That's Rule 14's evidence qualifier.
3. The "not because they are easy, but because they are hard" construction:
   admire it, read it aloud, and then don't use it in a blog. Once per career.
