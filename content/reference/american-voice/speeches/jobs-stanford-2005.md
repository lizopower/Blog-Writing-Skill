# "You've got to find what you love" — Steve Jobs, Stanford, 2005

> **Why this is the gold standard of plain American prose**
> - Vocabulary is almost entirely monosyllabic at the emotional peaks.
> - Structure announced in nine words, then honored exactly: three stories, no more.
> - Every abstraction is cashed out in an object: Coke bottles, a garage, a brick.
> - Contractions everywhere; zero corporate vocabulary from the CEO of two companies.

## Structure

Opening (3 sentences) → "Three stories" → connecting the dots (Reed, calligraphy,
Mac) → love and loss (fired from Apple, NeXT, Pixar) → death (diagnosis) →
Whole Earth Catalog close: "Stay hungry. Stay foolish."

## Excerpts and what to steal

Setup in nine flat words — no throat-clearing:

> Today I want to tell you three stories from my life. That's it. No big deal.
> Just three stories.

Concrete poverty, no self-pity adjectives:

> I didn't have a dorm room, so I slept on the floor in friends' rooms. I
> returned Coke bottles for the five-cent deposits to buy food with.

The turn, in short declaratives:

> But something slowly began to dawn on me: I still loved what I did. […] And
> so I decided to start over.

A hard sentence made bearable by plainness:

> Sometimes life's gonna hit you in the head with a brick. Don't lose faith.

**Transfer note**: the two-word imperative close ("Stay hungry. Stay foolish.")
works because 2,200 words earned it. In a blog, earn your punch sentences the
same way — setup first, punch after.
