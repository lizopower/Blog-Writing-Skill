# "This Is Water" — David Foster Wallace, Kenyon, 2005

> **Why it's here**
> - The most admired piece of conversational American prose of its generation:
>   the sound of a brilliant person talking naturally — which is exactly the
>   register gap between "correct English" and "native English."
> - Register mixing is the masterclass: high abstraction sits next to plain
>   talk ("you will be totally hosed") without whiplash. Native writers move
>   between registers; ESL writing tends to stay locked in one.
> - Genre self-awareness: he names the conventions of his own genre and then
>   uses them anyway — honesty about form builds trust.

## Excerpts and what to steal

The parable — 43 words, monosyllabic, perfect setup/payoff:

> There are these two young fish swimming along and they happen to meet an
> older fish swimming the other way, who nods at them and says "Morning, boys.
> How's the water?" And the two young fish swim on for a bit, and then
> eventually one of them looks over at the other and goes "What the hell is
> water?"

Thesis stated plainly, immediately after:

> The point of the fish story is merely that the most obvious, important
> realities are often the ones that are hardest to see and talk about.

Register mixing — abstraction closed with slang:

> Learning how to think really means learning how to exercise some control
> over how and what you think. […] Because if you cannot exercise this kind
> of choice in adult life, you will be totally hosed.

Hyper-concrete observational detail (the supermarket passage):

> …you have to manoeuvre your junky cart through all these other tired,
> hurried people with carts […] and you get told to "Have a nice day" in a
> voice that is the absolute voice of death.

**Transfer notes**:
1. Steal: the parable-then-plain-thesis opening (story ≤50 words, then "The
   point is…" in one sentence). Works for technical intros.
2. Steal: register mixing in moderation — one plain-talk landing after a dense
   technical paragraph re-engages the reader.
3. Do NOT steal: DFW's run-on maximalist sentences and et-cetera digressions.
   They're his signature, not a model for B2B prose. Rule 1's rhythm variance
   has a ceiling.
