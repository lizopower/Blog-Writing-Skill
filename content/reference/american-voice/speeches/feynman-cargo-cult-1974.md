# "Cargo Cult Science" — Richard Feynman, Caltech, 1974

> **Why it matters for this skill**
> - The purest example of a technical expert talking to peers in plain speech —
>   exactly the register `tech-blog-writer` targets.
> - Argument built from concrete anecdotes (South Sea runways, Millikan's oil
>   drops, Young's rats) that each earn one principle. Anecdote → mechanism →
>   rule. That's the skill's middle-paragraph pattern.
> - Its core theme — report everything that might make your result invalid —
>   is the philosophical basis for this skill's evidence-qualifier rule (Rule 14)
>   and fact-checker stage.

## Excerpts and what to steal

The central image, in flat sentences that get shorter as the point lands:

> They're doing everything right. The form is perfect. It looks exactly the way
> it looked before. But it doesn't work. No airplanes land.

Definition by consequence, not abstraction:

> It's a kind of scientific integrity, a principle of scientific thought that
> corresponds to a kind of utter honesty — a kind of leaning over backwards.

The most quoted engineering sentence of the 20th century — note the em-dash
used ONCE, for the reversal:

> The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the
> easiest person to fool.

Concrete instruction, no hedging:

> If you're doing an experiment, you should report everything that you think
> might make it invalid — not only what you think is right about it.

**Transfer note**: Feynman never says "importantly" or "it should be noted."
He puts the important thing in a short sentence and stops. Do that.
