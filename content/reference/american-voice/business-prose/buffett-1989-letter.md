# Buffett's 1989 Shareholder Letter — "Mistakes of the First Twenty-five Years"

> **Why Buffett letters are the closest match to B2B blog register**
> - Unlike the speeches, this IS written business prose for a reading audience —
>   complex financial logic in plain words, zero jargon, zero hedging.
> - The "Mistakes" section is the masterclass in authority-through-admission:
>   he builds trust by cataloging his own errors with numbers attached. This is
>   the skill's Brand Alignment principle (state conditions and constraints
>   plainly) executed at the highest level.
> - Aphorisms come AFTER evidence, never instead of it. Story first, one-liner
>   second — the one-liner is the receipt, not the claim.

## Excerpts and what to steal

Aphorism earned by a confessed mistake (the most famous line in the letter):

> It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair
> company at a wonderful price. Charlie understood this early; I was a slow
> learner.

Metaphor doing analytical work, then the plain restatement:

> Good jockeys will do well on good horses, but not on broken-down nags. […]
> when a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a
> reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that
> remains intact.

Anti-heroics as strategy — humility with a point:

> After 25 years of buying and supervising a great variety of businesses,
> Charlie and I have not learned how to solve difficult business problems.
> What we have learned is to avoid them. […] Overall, however, we've done
> better by avoiding dragons than by slaying them.

Naming an unnamed force, then a numbered plain-language list:

> …an unseen force that we might call "the institutional imperative." […]
> (1) As if governed by Newton's First Law of Motion, an institution will
> resist any change in its current direction; (2) Just as work expands to fill
> available time, corporate projects or acquisitions will materialize to soak
> up available funds…

Humor by borrowed quotation, at his own expense:

> My behavior has matched that admitted by Mae West: "I was Snow White, but I
> drifted."

**Transfer notes**:
1. The admit-your-mistakes structure transfers directly to B2B: a "what we got
   wrong in early deployments" section outperforms any capability claim.
2. Coin a term for an unnamed phenomenon ("the institutional imperative"),
   define it with a numbered list, then use it — instant thought leadership,
   and AEO-quotable (Rule 15 definition pattern).
3. Analogies must do analytical work (jockeys/horses maps exactly onto
   management/economics). Decoration analogies get cut.
4. Further reading: every letter 1977–present is free at
   berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html. The 1996 "Owner's Manual" and
   2007 "moats" discussion are equally strong.
