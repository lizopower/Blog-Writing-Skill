## Representative excerpts

Definition-first opening (Rule 15 exemplar):

> Injection molding tolerances are acceptable variations in size for part
> features and overall dimensions. They are expressed as plus or minus (±)
> values in millimeters or inches and vary by the type of material that's used
> and the overall size of the part.

Cost stake, stated plainly:

> As a rule, parts with tighter tolerances are more expensive because they
> have stricter tooling and processing requirements.

Concrete example over abstraction (stack-ups):

> Consider the example of three injection molded parts that are bolted
> together. Each part has a hole with tolerances within the dimensions you
> specify. That's great, but all three holes still need to align so that a
> fastener can pass through them.

Cause-effect warning, no hedging:

> Out-of-tolerance molds result in out-of-tolerance parts. That's why during
> the CNC machining of tools, mold makers must control tool dimensions.
> Typically, injection molds are machined to tolerances of ±0.127 mm.

FAQ answer with entity echo + numbers (Rule 16/18 pattern):

> For non-critical applications like consumer products, injection molding
> tolerances are typically ±0.1 mm. For applications that require tighter
> tolerances, such as injection molded medical parts, ±0.025 mm is typical
> instead.
